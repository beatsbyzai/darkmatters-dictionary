Introduction This is the Dictionary of Dark Matters. 186 During the Summer of 2020, Dark Matters was taught by American Artist and Zainab Aliyu (TA). For ten weeks, we stewarded and taught this class over the video platform Zoom. 559 Thirty students convened over and through national borders to study 482 alongside us and with one another. We engaged in intimate practices of unlearning, deep listening and reflection. Most importantly, we studied “under the university,” a framing we borrow from theorist s Fred Moten and Stefano Harney.

The class, titled Dark Matters: On Blackness, Surveillance, 487 and the Whiteness of the Screen, takes it's namesake from Simone Browne's book Dark Matters: On the Surveillance of Blackness. This critical text winds personal, political and pop-cultural narratives of racial gaze 442 and sousveillance 476 to address the origins of so many recurring dynamics of power that operate through high technology. The readings in our class began with Wendy Chun's critique of software 473 and ideology's 313 tendencies towards obfuscation, 386 as well as American Artist's Black Gooey Universe 100 and imagining of an unsovereign technology. Over the four sessions that this class has been taught, we have introduced authors to the curriculum such as Jackie Wang, David N. Pellow, Lisa Sun-Hee Park and Ruha Benjamin. Selected work by these authors hold the origins of the interface 100 to account for the continuation of racial capitalism, 100 the debt state, 100 colonialism, 100 the white racial frame, 100 and other global practices that are deeply rooted in American colonial history.

Dark Matters reminds us about the panoptic 100 models and codes that construct the world we live in, their underlying ideologies, as well as the need for our consistent reckoning. 100 This class has given us intentional time to tend to ourselves as we contend with this predatory state, 100 to study the material while existing within the systems we are learning to name. Together, we studied theory, but theory is nothing without the lived experiences that inform it. The duration of our time together coincided with the COVID -19 pandemic, the ongoing movement for Black Lives (particularly in response to the murder of George Floyd), and the calling out of various academic institutions and places of work (including our own) for their perpetuation of anti-blackness. 100 These issues framed all of our conversations and largely informed our communal ethic 100 of shar ing, creat ing and relat ing with one an other. Through our co-learning, we can better understand how structures are compromised, and we can begin to imagine strategies for future resistance, and what an outside to the structures we participate in might actually look and feel like.

The self-published book you are reading is the culmination of all of that thought. It is a 500+ page (and growing) collaborative people's dictionary 100 written over the course of ten weeks. It is an abundance 100 of poetry, prose, creative writing, personal history and illustration filled with terms we have come across during our time together. For transparency, 100 every voice within the dictionary is uniquely represented with the contributor's preference in typography. By archiving our histories and inserting our human 100 experiences into the critical theories we are learning, we are looking inwardly and actively engaging with the way we move through the world. It feels increasingly necessary, helping us to ask not only about the technologies, processes and policies that govern civil liberties, but also about whose bodies and freedoms remain captive.

— contributed by American Aritst and Zainab Aliyu
